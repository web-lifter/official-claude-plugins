**Date:** 11/04/2026
**Prepared by:** Brand strategist (via Claude `logo-brief` skill)
**Prepared for:** Independent designer (Fiverr Pro / 99designs)
**Budget:** AUD $2,500–$4,000
**Timeline:** Brief sent Apr 11; final delivery by May 16

---

## 1. Background

### About the brand
Verdant is a Melbourne-based eco cleaning brand selling concentrated, plant-based household cleaners in refillable glass bottles. The brand is built on a single contrarian idea: cleaning products don't need to look or smell like a chemistry lab, and they don't need to ship water around the world in single-use plastic. Verdant ships dry concentrate; the customer adds tap water at home. The product line is small (5 SKUs) and the brand sells direct-to-consumer via subscription.

### Stage
New brand. Pre-launch (no public-facing assets yet beyond a holding page).

### Existing recognition
None. The founder previously ran a sustainability consultancy under a different name; that brand is being retired.

---

## 2. Goals for this logo

1. **Stand out in a category dominated by sterile clinical packaging** — most eco cleaners use sans-serif logos and pale green colours that fade into the supermarket shelf.
2. **Work as both a Shopify favicon (16px) and a printed bottle label (12mm)** at minimum size.
3. **Communicate craft and naturalness** without using clichés (no leaves bursting through letters, no gradient greens, no "earth-loving" iconography).

---

## 3. Audience

- **Primary audience:** Australian women aged 28–45, urban, professional or self-employed, household income AUD $90k+, already buying organic groceries and avoiding single-use plastic. They are sceptical of greenwashing and read ingredient lists.
- **Secondary audience:** Eco-conscious gift-buyers (the bottles are gift-worthy). Hospitality businesses (cafés, boutique hotels) buying for refilling.
- **Cultural/regional context:** Australia. Avoid green/yellow combinations (too closely associated with cleaning/chemical products like Mr. Sheen). Avoid "Australian wildlife" iconography (kangaroos, koalas) — feels touristy.

---

## 4. Brand identity reference

(From `brand-identity` skill output, 09/04/2026)

- **Mission:** "We make the cleanest cleaning products on the planet — both literally and visually."
- **Voice attributes:** Direct (not blunt), Considered (not pretentious), Warm (not cute), Honest (not preachy)
- **Archetype:** Sage with a Caregiver secondary
- **Anti-brand:**
  - We are not preachy environmentalism (no "save the planet" copy)
  - We are not budget eco (we are mid-premium, $18–28 per concentrate)
  - We are not lab-clinical (no test tubes, no chemistry symbols)
  - We are not folk-craft hand-drawn (no twigs and string)

## 5. Mark Type

**Recommended mark type:** Combination mark (Wordmark + simple botanical pictorial)

**Why this type:**
Verdant is a new brand with no recognition, so a pure pictorial mark would be incomprehensible. A pure wordmark would not register on a packed supermarket shelf at 3 metres. A combination mark — wordmark + a small, restrained botanical pictorial — gives the brand both legibility (at distance, the pictorial registers) and clarity (close up, the name reads). The pictorial element should be subtle enough that it can stand alone as the product-bottle stamp at 12mm.

**Alternative considered:** Pictorial alone — rejected because new brand recognition is too weak.

## 6. Concept Directions

### Concept 1 — "The Single Leaf"

**Visual description:** A single, drawn-not-rendered leaf paired with a serif wordmark "verdant" set in lowercase. The leaf is one continuous line, no fill, in the brand primary green. The leaf sits to the left of the wordmark in horizontal lockup, and above the wordmark in stacked lockup. Leaf is the product stamp at small sizes.

**Style references:**
- Aesop (restraint, lowercase serif wordmark)
- Frank Body (restraint, single serif word)
- The early Sweetgreen wordmark (single leaf, restrained)

**Why it fits:** Verdant's Sage archetype calls for considered restraint, not visual energy. A single leaf avoids the "burst of leaves" cliché while still signalling botanical. Lowercase serif is approachable but considered.

**Risks:** The "single leaf" idea is the most obvious botanical concept and could feel safe. Mitigated by the specific stylisation (one continuous line, no fill).

**AI prompt seed:**
```
A minimalist logo of a single eucalyptus leaf, drawn as one continuous flowing line,
no fill, sage green on white, paired with the lowercase wordmark "verdant" in a
classical serif typeface inspired by Garamond, horizontal lockup, vector flat,
1:1 aspect, --no gradients --no shadows --no 3d --no realistic detail
```

---

### Concept 2 — "The Drop"

**Visual description:** A perfect water droplet shape, drawn as one continuous outline (no fill), paired with the wordmark "verdant" in the same typeface as Concept 1. The drop is the dominant element — referencing Verdant's "just add water" product format. The wordmark sits beneath the drop in stacked lockup. The drop alone serves as the favicon.

**Style references:**
- Method Soap (water droplet inspired silhouettes — but Method's marks are rendered, this should be flat)
- Pukka Tea (single iconic mark + serif wordmark)
- early Innocent Drinks (simple iconic mark + lowercase wordmark)

**Why it fits:** Verdant's product format is unique in the category — dry concentrate, customer adds water. The water drop is conceptually accurate (not just decorative). It's also distinctive in the eco-cleaning category, which is dominated by leaves/plants.

**Risks:** Water droplet is a common logo motif and risks feeling generic. Distinctiveness depends entirely on the execution (line weight, proportions, drawing style).

**AI prompt seed:**
```
A minimalist logo of a single water droplet, single continuous line outline only,
asymmetric organic shape, sage green on white, vector flat, paired with lowercase
serif wordmark "verdant" stacked beneath, 1:1, --no gradients --no shadows --no fill
```

---

### Concept 3 — "The Stamp"

**Visual description:** A circular emblem/stamp containing the lowercase wordmark "verdant" in a classical serif, with a thin botanical line (single sprig of leaves) integrated into the circle. The stamp reads as both a maker's mark and an apothecary label. The stamp shape is the favicon and the bottle stamp; a horizontal wordmark version exists for headers and digital.

**Style references:**
- Aesop product labels (circular stamp containing serif type)
- Le Labo (apothecary stamp)
- early Glossier (clean circular mark)

**Why it fits:** Verdant is positioned as mid-premium, not budget eco. The apothecary-stamp visual language signals craft, restraint, and seriousness about ingredients — all aligned with the Sage archetype. Stamps are also intrinsically packaging-friendly.

**Risks:** Emblems are detail-heavy at small sizes; the circular stamp may need a simplified favicon variant. Also, the "apothecary stamp" is a recognisable visual pattern in the natural-products category, which could read as derivative if not executed with distinction.

**AI prompt seed:**
```
A minimalist circular apothecary stamp logo, lowercase wordmark "verdant" set in
elegant serif Garamond-style typography centered inside, with a single thin botanical
sprig integrated at the top of the circle, sage green on white, vector flat, 1:1,
--no gradients --no shadows --no 3d --no realistic detail
```

## 7. Style Direction

### Typography (for the wordmark in all concepts)
- **Style:** Old Style Serif
- **Reference typefaces:** Garamond Premier, Sabon, Tiempos Text
- **Weight:** Regular
- **Letter spacing:** Slightly expanded (track +20)
- **Customisation:** The lowercase "v" should have a slight customisation — extended ascender or distinctive terminal — to make the wordmark distinctive. Designer's call.

### Form
- **Style:** Organic but disciplined (drawn shapes, not free-hand wobble)
- **Line weight:** Medium-thin (consistent across the mark)
- **Detail level:** Minimal — the mark must read at 12mm

### Colour
- **Number of colours:** 1 (the brand primary green) + black for type
- **Primary colour:** `#3F5E3A` (deep forest green, *not* the bright lime green of typical eco brands)
- **Secondary colour:** None — the brand uses a wide neutral ramp instead of a second colour
- **Single-colour reproduction strategy:** Mark and wordmark both work in pure black on cream packaging and pure white on dark backgrounds. Forest green is for digital and select print only.

## 10. References

### Positive references
1. **Aesop** — admired for the restraint of the wordmark, the lowercase serif, and the way the brand identity sits in a single confident piece of typography. The product labels are templates for what restraint can do.
2. **Le Labo** — admired for the apothecary-stamp visual language and the way the brand looks expensive without being decorative.
3. **Frank Body** — admired for the lowercase wordmark and for proving that an Australian DTC brand can compete on visual identity in a crowded category.
4. **Pukka Tea** — admired for the way a botanical pictorial works as both a small stamp (on tea bags) and a hero element (on packaging).
5. **Wild deodorant** — admired for the eco-product proof that a brand can be both restrained and warm.

### Negative references
1. **Method Soap (current logo)** — too playful, too rendered. We are not "fun cleaning."
2. **Earth Choice (Australian eco brand)** — generic green/yellow palette, sans-serif, blends into supermarket shelves. We are not budget eco.
3. **Ecover** — feels dated. The leaf/water-drop combination has been done.
4. **The Body Shop** — feels late-1990s. We are not "natural beauty."

### Avoid list (do NOT include)
- Gradients of any kind
- Drop shadows or any 3D effect
- Stock-art leaves bursting through type
- Multiple leaves or floral arrangements
- Test tubes, beakers, chemistry icons
- Hand-drawn / handwritten / "casual" elements
- Sun rays, sunbursts, or "energy" visual cues
- Globe / earth imagery
- More than two colours
- Sans-serif typography (we are not a tech brand)
- The colour combinations green+yellow, green+brown, or pastel green
- "Eco", "natural", "pure" in any tagline within the lockup
