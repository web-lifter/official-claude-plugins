## 1. Jung's 12 Brand Archetypes

Each archetype has a core desire, a goal, a fear, and a strategy. When mapping a brand, evidence from Phase 1 (founding story, customer truth, competitive position) should drive the choice — not aesthetic preference.

### 1.1 The Innocent
- **Core desire:** Safety, happiness
- **Goal:** Be happy
- **Fear:** Being punished for doing something wrong
- **Strategy:** Do things right
- **Voice:** Optimistic, honest, humble, simple
- **Example brands:** Coca-Cola, Dove, Aveeno
- **Shadow:** Naïve, in denial about problems
- **Best for:** Wellness, food, family-oriented brands

### 1.2 The Sage
- **Core desire:** Truth, understanding
- **Goal:** Use intelligence and analysis to understand the world
- **Fear:** Being duped, misled, or ignorant
- **Strategy:** Seek out information and knowledge; reflect
- **Voice:** Considered, thoughtful, accurate, measured
- **Example brands:** Google, BBC, The Economist, Audible
- **Shadow:** Detached, paralysed by analysis, condescending
- **Best for:** Education, research, advisory, journalism

### 1.3 The Explorer
- **Core desire:** Freedom, discovery
- **Goal:** Experience a better, more authentic, more fulfilling life
- **Fear:** Getting trapped, conformity
- **Strategy:** Journey, seek out new experiences, escape
- **Voice:** Independent, restless, adventurous
- **Example brands:** Patagonia, Jeep, The North Face, Indiana Jones
- **Shadow:** Aimless, perpetual outsider, unable to commit
- **Best for:** Outdoor, travel, individualist tools

### 1.4 The Outlaw (Rebel)
- **Core desire:** Revolution, liberation
- **Goal:** Overturn what isn't working
- **Fear:** Being powerless, ineffectual
- **Strategy:** Disrupt, destroy, shock
- **Voice:** Defiant, rebellious, raw
- **Example brands:** Harley-Davidson, Virgin, Diesel, Liquid Death
- **Shadow:** Destruction for its own sake, alienating
- **Best for:** Counter-culture, disruption-led, anti-establishment

### 1.5 The Magician
- **Core desire:** Transformation
- **Goal:** Make dreams come true
- **Fear:** Unintended negative consequences
- **Strategy:** Develop a vision and live by it
- **Voice:** Visionary, mystical, transformative
- **Example brands:** Apple, Disney, Tesla, Polaroid
- **Shadow:** Manipulative, hollow promises
- **Best for:** Tech, entertainment, transformation services

### 1.6 The Hero
- **Core desire:** Mastery
- **Goal:** Improve the world through courage and skill
- **Fear:** Weakness, vulnerability
- **Strategy:** Be as strong and competent as possible
- **Voice:** Confident, determined, inspiring
- **Example brands:** Nike, BMW, FedEx, US Marines
- **Shadow:** Arrogant, in constant battle
- **Best for:** Fitness, performance, professional services

### 1.7 The Lover
- **Core desire:** Intimacy, connection
- **Goal:** Be in relationship with the people, work, and surroundings they love
- **Fear:** Being alone, unwanted, unloved
- **Strategy:** Become more attractive — physically, emotionally, intellectually
- **Voice:** Passionate, sensual, warm, indulgent
- **Example brands:** Chanel, Godiva, Victoria's Secret, Häagen-Dazs
- **Shadow:** Codependent, vain, jealous
- **Best for:** Beauty, luxury, romance, food

### 1.8 The Jester
- **Core desire:** Joy, fun
- **Goal:** Have a great time and lighten up the world
- **Fear:** Being bored or boring others
- **Strategy:** Play, make jokes, be funny
- **Voice:** Playful, witty, irreverent
- **Example brands:** Old Spice, Skittles, M&Ms, Aldi (AU ads)
- **Shadow:** Frivolous, irresponsible, hurtful
- **Best for:** Snack food, entertainment, casual consumer goods

### 1.9 The Everyman (Regular Guy)
- **Core desire:** Connection, belonging
- **Goal:** To belong, fit in
- **Fear:** Standing out, being excluded
- **Strategy:** Develop ordinary, solid virtues; common touch
- **Voice:** Friendly, down-to-earth, unpretentious
- **Example brands:** IKEA, eBay, Bunnings (AU), Toyota
- **Shadow:** Lacks distinction, conformist
- **Best for:** Mass-market retail, household, accessible services

### 1.10 The Caregiver
- **Core desire:** To protect and care for others
- **Goal:** Help others
- **Fear:** Selfishness, ingratitude
- **Strategy:** Do things for others
- **Voice:** Warm, reassuring, generous, nurturing
- **Example brands:** Volvo, Johnson & Johnson, Médecins Sans Frontières
- **Shadow:** Martyrdom, enabling, controlling
- **Best for:** Healthcare, insurance, education, parenting

### 1.11 The Ruler
- **Core desire:** Control
- **Goal:** Create a prosperous, successful family or community
- **Fear:** Chaos, being overthrown
- **Strategy:** Exercise power
- **Voice:** Authoritative, refined, confident, exclusive
- **Example brands:** Rolex, Mercedes-Benz, Microsoft (enterprise), American Express
- **Shadow:** Authoritarian, rigid, controlling
- **Best for:** Luxury, premium B2B, status goods

### 1.12 The Creator
- **Core desire:** To create things of enduring value
- **Goal:** Realise a vision
- **Fear:** Mediocre vision or execution
- **Strategy:** Develop artistic control and skill
- **Voice:** Imaginative, expressive, original
- **Example brands:** LEGO, Adobe, Crayola, Etsy
- **Shadow:** Perfectionist, unable to ship, self-indulgent
- **Best for:** Creative tools, design, craft, content platforms

## 2. Common Archetype Blends

Most strong brands aren't pure single archetypes — they're a primary with a secondary that adds nuance.

| Blend | Effect | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Sage + Hero | The competent expert who helps you win | LinkedIn Learning, Strava |
| Magician + Creator | Transformative tools for makers | Apple, Figma |
| Caregiver + Sage | Wise, trustworthy guide | NHS, Mayo Clinic |
| Outlaw + Hero | Disruptor with conviction | Tesla, Liquid Death |
| Jester + Everyman | Fun, accessible, never pretentious | Aldi (AU), Innocent Drinks |
| Lover + Magician | Indulgent transformation | Lush, Aesop |
| Explorer + Creator | The artist seeking new territory | Patagonia, A24 |

When picking a blend, the primary should account for ~70% of the brand voice and the secondary should colour the remaining 30%.

## 3. Voice Attribute Library

Common voice attributes, with the "this not that" pairing that makes them specific:

| Attribute | This (good) | Not that (avoid) |
|---|---|---|
| Direct | Confident, gets to the point | Blunt, cold, abrupt |
| Warm | Genuinely cares | Saccharine, performative |
| Confident | Clear conviction, takes a stance | Arrogant, dismissive |
| Witty | Smart and a little playful | Trying too hard, pun-heavy |
| Calm | Measured, doesn't panic | Boring, monotone |
| Bold | Willing to make claims | Hyperbolic, "world-changing" |
| Technical | Uses precise language | Jargon-heavy, exclusionary |
| Plainspoken | Anyone can understand | Dumbed down, condescending |
| Curious | Asks questions, explores | Wishy-washy, indecisive |
| Honest | Tells uncomfortable truths | Blunt, oversharing |

A voice attribute that doesn't have a clear "not that" twin is too vague to be useful.

## 4. Banned Generic Values

These values appear on >80% of corporate "About Us" pages. Reject them unless the user can prove specific, observable behaviour that justifies the choice:

- Innovation
- Integrity
- Excellence
- Customer focus / customer-first / customer-centric
- Teamwork / collaboration
- Passion
- Quality
- Respect
- Trust
- Communication

If the user insists on one of these, dig deeper: *what specifically* about how they work makes this true? Then write the value in a way that captures the specific behaviour, not the generic word. Example: "Integrity" → "We refund without arguing." That's a value. "Integrity" is a placeholder.

## 5. Example Value Statements (Strong vs Weak)

| Weak (generic) | Strong (specific) |
|---|---|
| Customer focus | We answer every email within 4 hours, even on weekends. |
| Innovation | We rebuild our product from scratch every 18 months. |
| Quality | We refuse to ship until two engineers have signed off. |
| Integrity | We refund without arguing. |
| Excellence | We benchmark against the best 1% in our category, not the median. |
| Teamwork | We don't have job titles in customer-facing emails. |
| Transparency | We publish our salary bands, churn rate, and revenue publicly. |
| Sustainability | We refuse contracts from fossil-fuel clients, even profitable ones. |

The pattern: the strong version is something a competitor could legitimately disagree with. If no competitor would disagree, it's not a value — it's a platitude.

## 6. Tone Slider Templates

Standard 4-dimension tone matrix. The brand defines its default position on each slider and the conditions under which it flexes.

```
Formality:  Casual ●——————————○ Formal
Energy:     Calm   ○————●———————— Energetic
Humour:     Serious ○——————————● Playful
Warmth:     Reserved ○————●—————— Warm
```

**Flex examples:**
- "Default warmth, but more reserved in legal/billing communications"
- "Default casual, but more formal when addressing enterprise customers"
- "Default playful in social, but serious in customer-support resolution"

## 7. Anti-Brand Statement Patterns

The 5 "we are not" statements should each cover a different category:

1. **Aesthetic anti-brand** — what the brand doesn't look like ("we don't use stock photography")
2. **Voice anti-brand** — what the brand doesn't sound like ("we don't use exclamation marks")
3. **Audience anti-brand** — who the brand isn't for ("we are not for enterprise IT departments")
4. **Position anti-brand** — what category position the brand rejects ("we are not the cheap option")
5. **Behavioural anti-brand** — what the brand refuses to do ("we don't do upsell pop-ups")

Specifying each category prevents the anti-brand from being all-aesthetic or all-positioning.

## 8. Output Validation Checklist

Before finalising the brand identity document, verify:

- [ ] Mission is one sentence, present tense, with verb + outcome + audience + impact
- [ ] Vision is a future-state statement, not a goal
- [ ] All 3–5 values are specific (not on the banned list)
- [ ] Each value has a behavioural test
- [ ] Each voice attribute has a do/don't pair
- [ ] Tone sliders all have a default position
- [ ] Voice is shown in 4 contexts (UI, landing, support, social)
- [ ] Primary archetype is justified with Phase 1 evidence
- [ ] Anti-brand has 5 statements covering different categories
- [ ] Open Questions section flags any thin-evidence assumptions
