# Competitive Dynamics — AU food-delivery (two-sided marketplace; Uber Eats / DoorDash / Menulog / direct)

**Date:** 20/05/2026
**Time horizon:** 24 months
**Our position:** Incumbent restaurant brand (mid-size, 30 locations) — buyer of marketplace services + operator of direct ordering

---

## Market Definition

- **Narrow scope:** Two-sided marketplaces matching restaurants with consumers for hot-food delivery in AU metropolitan areas
- **Two sides scored separately:** supply-side (restaurants) and demand-side (consumers)
- **Excluded:** Grocery delivery (Coles/Woolies own-channel); subscription meal kits; in-store dine-in
- **Players (Q1 2026):** Uber Eats (~52% gross orders), DoorDash (~24%), Menulog/JustEat (~16%), Deliveroo (exited AU 2022), direct + Order-with-Google / Instagram orders (~8% and growing)

---

## 5 Forces — Supply Side (restaurants)

| Force | Score (1–5) | Evidence |
|-------|------------|----------|
| Rivalry among existing platforms | 5 | Three at scale; constant commission-rate battles; Uber Eats + DoorDash bundle ad spend |
| Threat of new entrants (platform-side) | 1 | High capital + driver-network barriers; Deliveroo exited; few have $500M+ to spend |
| Bargaining power of buyers (restaurants like us) | 2 | Restaurants are price-takers on commission (~25–35%); cannot meaningfully negotiate without multi-state scale |
| Bargaining power of suppliers (drivers) | 4 | Driver supply highly elastic; gig-work regulation tightening in AU (Fair Work decisions 2024+) shifting cost structure |
| Threat of substitutes (direct order, Order-with-Google) | 4 | Growing fast; commission ~3% Stripe vs 25–35% platform — economic gravity is real |
| **Sum (supply side)** | **16/25** | Brutal for restaurants; favourable for platforms |

## 5 Forces — Demand Side (consumers)

| Force | Score (1–5) | Evidence |
|-------|------------|----------|
| Rivalry | 5 | Platforms run promo wars; consumer-facing CAC > LTV per cohort in non-mature markets |
| Threat of new entrants | 1 | Same as above |
| Bargaining power of consumers | 4 | Multi-homing common; price-sensitive cohort grows with cost-of-living pressure |
| Bargaining power of suppliers (restaurants) | 3 | Big restaurant chains can demand revenue-share concessions; small restaurants cannot |
| Threat of substitutes (cooking; pickup; subscription meal kits; supermarket prepared meals) | 3 | Increasing — Coles/Woolies meal range has displaced lower-tier delivery |
| **Sum (demand side)** | **16/25** | Same magnitude; different mechanics |

## Equilibrium Prediction (24 months)

- **Predicted dynamic:** Duopoly stabilisation — Uber Eats + DoorDash consolidate to ~80% combined share; Menulog/JustEat continues to lose share to direct + Order-with-Google
- **Players consolidating:** Possible Menulog AU sale or exit by end of 2027; DoorDash + Uber Eats both stable
- **Players growing:** Direct ordering channels (Order-with-Google / Stripe + Bopple / Order Up / DoorDash Drive); restaurant-direct loyalty apps
- **Our trajectory (as restaurant):** If we maintain 60% via platforms + 40% direct, we improve unit economics by ~$3-5 per order. The shift is rational and underway.
- **Confidence:** Medium-high — the economic gravity of the commission gap (25-35% vs 3%) creates a strong pull toward direct

---

## Response-Game Tree

### If we move first — shift to 60% direct by raising platform pricing 8%

| Our move | Likely competitor response | Our counter | Equilibrium |
|----------|---------------------------|-------------|-------------|
| Raise platform-shown menu prices 8% to offset commission; keep direct prices unchanged | Other restaurants follow within 12 months (incentive to do the same is too obvious) | None needed | New equilibrium: platform-shown prices 8% higher across the board; consumers shift to direct ~15%; platforms attempt counter via increased subsidies (limited budget) |

### If platforms move first — DoorDash raises commission from 28% to 32%

| Their move | Our response | Their counter | Equilibrium |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| DoorDash commission goes from 28% to 32% | We re-raise menu prices 4% on their platform only; consider channel-only delisting at low-margin locations | DoorDash offers individual-restaurant rebate to retain; we accept if rebate is > 3pp | Cost passed to consumer; demand elasticity captures ~half of the increase; net loss of order volume to direct |

**Nash equilibrium:** Both sides keep raising consumer-facing prices; consumers respond by mixing direct + multi-homing. Long-term loser: small restaurants without direct-channel infrastructure. Long-term winner: well-capitalised restaurant chains (us, McDonald's, Hungry Jack's) + direct-tech enablers (Stripe, Order-with-Google).

## Red Team — Where this analysis is wrong

### Too optimistic

1. **"Direct channel will hit 40% of order volume in 24 months"** — only if our consumer-facing app + ordering UX is good. Most mid-size chains have invested in this and seen *single-digit* direct share. The 40% target assumes ad spend + UX investment of $300k+. If we under-invest, direct stalls at 12% and the analysis is wrong.
2. **"Commission floor will hold at 25%+"** — possibly. But Order-with-Google is a hidden 3rd platform that could shift the floor. If Google decides to operationalise food delivery as a margin-zero service to drive search dominance, all bets are off.

### Counter-moves the analysis doesn't account for

1. **Uber Eats locks out direct ordering** — exclusive contracts with restaurants (especially small ones). Already happens in some segments.
2. **Government intervention** — ACCC has been probing platform-restaurant commission rates. A regulated commission cap (say 20%) would shift entire economics.
3. **DoorDash + Uber Eats merger** — politically unlikely but financially logical. Would create a near-monopoly. ACCC would block but only after months of damage.

### Disconfirmers — if observed in 12 months, this analysis is wrong

1. Commission rates rise above 35% (current ~25-35%): supply-side power is even worse than scored
2. Order-with-Google captures > 15% of AU orders: platform dynamics radically different
3. Two of three platforms exit AU: oligopoly → monopoly; commission rises sharply
4. Direct channel exceeds 50% of orders for any major chain: equilibrium shifted; platform pricing power broken

### Time-horizon risks

- **Year 1:** Stable duopoly; direct channel growth steady
- **Year 3:** Either ACCC ruling reshapes commissions OR direct channel hits inflection — pivotal evidence
- **Year 5:** Most-likely outcomes: (a) duopoly + 30% direct, (b) regulated commissions, (c) Order-with-Google as third major player

### The base case I'd hold the operator to

"We are paying 25-35% of order revenue to platforms. The 'fair' equilibrium price is closer to 15-20% in a competitive market — we're being taxed. Direct ordering is the rational response, but achieving > 40% direct share requires sustained capital investment in UX + loyalty + marketing that most operators don't make. The expected outcome over 24 months: we maintain 60/40 platform/direct, save ~$3M/yr on commissions, but lose ~$500k/yr in marketing that we wouldn't otherwise spend. Net benefit ~$2.5M. Worth doing. But predict only modest improvement — not transformation."

---

## Strategic Implications

1. **Invest in direct channel infrastructure now.** UX, loyalty, marketing. The 25-35% commission gap is structural; capture it.
2. **Do not bet on platform commission caps.** ACCC investigation is slow; assume current rates persist for 24 months minimum.
3. **Watch Order-with-Google quarterly.** If it captures > 10% share, the dynamics shift radically.
4. **Multi-home strategically — don't delist from any platform yet.** Volume from the marginal platform is still profitable; the math is "should I be on Menulog?" not "should I be on Uber Eats?"
5. **Consider a small-chain coalition** — if 20+ small chains coordinated direct-channel infrastructure investment, individual costs drop and consumer brand recognition rises. Probably illegal under cartel law as currently framed; legal review needed.
