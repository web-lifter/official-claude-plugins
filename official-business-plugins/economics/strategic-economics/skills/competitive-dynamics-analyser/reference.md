# Competitive Dynamics Analyser — Reference Material

## 5 Forces Scoring Rubric

### Rivalry among existing competitors

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 (low) | Few players (1–2); differentiated products; growing market |
| 3 | 3–5 players; some differentiation; stable market |
| 5 (high) | Many players; commoditised; declining market; price-war episodes; aggressive feature parity |

### Threat of new entrants

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 (low) | High capital req; regulatory barriers; strong network effects; high switching costs |
| 3 | Medium barriers; possible but expensive |
| 5 (high) | Low capital; no regulation; easy distribution; copycat products |

### Bargaining power of buyers

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 (low) | Many small buyers; low switching ease; high product differentiation |
| 3 | Mixed; some concentrated buyers |
| 5 (high) | Few concentrated buyers (e.g. Coles+Woolies = 70% retail); commoditised product |

### Bargaining power of suppliers

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 (low) | Many substitutable suppliers; commodity inputs |
| 3 | Some critical suppliers; switching possible |
| 5 (high) | One/few dominant suppliers; high switching cost; supplier integration vertical |

### Threat of substitutes

| Score | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 (low) | No substitutes; unique value-add |
| 3 | Some substitutes but quality/cost gap |
| 5 (high) | Multiple cheap substitutes; switching is easy; substitutes improving fast |

## Equilibrium Pattern Library

### Cournot (quantity competition)
- Players choose output simultaneously; price is determined by total supply
- Common in commodities, capacity-constrained industries
- Profits positive; not perfectly competitive

### Bertrand (price competition)
- Players choose price simultaneously
- Pure form → price = marginal cost (race to the bottom)
- Differentiation softens
- Common in undifferentiated SaaS, hosting, telecoms

### Dominant firm with competitive fringe
- One major player (40%+ share), several smaller
- Dominant sets price/strategy; fringe adapts
- Stable; smaller players survive on niches
- Common in: AU accounting (Xero), search (Google), AU supermarkets (Coles+Woolies)

### Collusive / cartel
- Players coordinate (explicitly illegal in AU; implicitly common via market signalling)
- Higher prices; lower output
- Unstable — defection incentive
- Watch for: synchronous pricing, "leadership" patterns

### Winner-take-all
- Network effects + switching costs lock in single dominant player
- Most other players exit or pivot
- Common in: social platforms, marketplaces

### Fragmented / monopolistic competition
- Many players; light differentiation; modest pricing power
- Local markets, cafés, hairdressers
- Hard to scale without re-defining

## Common Counter-Move Patterns

| Our move | Common competitor responses |
|----------|----------------------------|
| Price cut | Match (within weeks if pockets allow); bundle to obscure; cut elsewhere |
| Feature launch | Copy in 6–12 months; differentiate on adjacent feature |
| Vertical expansion | Defend territory aggressively; possibly counter-invade |
| Geographic expansion | Cheaper to defend home than attack new region — depending on logistics |
| M&A | Counter-M&A; price-war the combined entity |
| Channel partnership | Form competing partnership; lock in via exclusivity |
| Free tier launch | Match free tier; or differentiate on something the free can't reach |

## Exit Scenario Indicators

A competitor is likely to exit if:

- Cash runway < 12 months + no recent raise
- Major executive departures (especially CRO / VP Sales)
- Pulled funding round / down-round
- Pricing changes that suggest desperation (heavy discounting, "free for 6 months")
- Pivoting messaging away from the core market
- Geographic retreat
- Strategic-buyer rumours

## Australian-Specific Considerations

- **Small market.** Many "global" patterns don't apply at AU scale (e.g. winner-take-all may be 2 players, not 1).
- **Regulatory bias toward incumbents.** ACMA / ACCC / ASIC frameworks often preserve incumbents.
- **High concentration in retail / banking / supermarkets.** 5 Forces analysis here always shows brutal buyer power.
- **Geographic distribution.** Concentration in capital cities; rural / regional often unserved → niche entry opportunity.
- **Distance from US/EU.** Some global players don't bother with AU — opens local opportunity, but also means smaller TAM.
