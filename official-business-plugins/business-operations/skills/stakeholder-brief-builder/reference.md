# Stakeholder Brief Builder — Reference Material

## SCQA Framework (Barbara Minto — Pyramid Principle)

SCQA is the structural backbone of executive communication. It ensures the reader can follow the logic without effort.

### The Four Elements

| Element | Purpose | Length | Typical opener |
|---------|---------|--------|---------------|
| **Situation** | Shared context — what both parties know to be true | 1–2 sentences | "As of [date]..." / "Since [event]..." |
| **Complication** | What has changed or is now threatening the situation | 1–2 sentences | "However..." / "Recently..." / "As a result..." |
| **Question** | The implicit question the complication raises (often not written) | 1 sentence | "So, what should we do?" / "How will this affect us?" |
| **Answer** | The direct answer — the "so what" — stated first | 1–3 sentences | "We are..." / "The board should approve..." / "Effective [date]..." |

### Why SCQA Works

The human brain processes information most efficiently when it knows the conclusion first and can use it as a frame for evaluating the supporting evidence. SCQA provides that frame immediately. Readers who do not read beyond the headline still understand the core message.

### SCQA vs Traditional Structure

| Traditional (bad for executives) | SCQA (effective for executives) |
|----------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Context → Context → Context → Recommendation | Answer → Context → Evidence → Action |
| Reader must wait until the end to know the point | Reader knows the point in the first sentence |
| Writer-centric | Reader-centric |

## Audience Mode Adaptation Matrix

### Executive Mode (Board, Investors)

- **Lead with**: Recommendation or conclusion (the Answer)
- **Quantify**: Every claim must have a number attached
- **Risk framing**: Risks first, then mitigation — do not soften
- **Language**: Precise, impersonal, no hedging
- **Structure**: TL;DR (3 bullets) → Headline → Evidence → Decision ask → Risks
- **What they fear**: Being caught off-guard; governance failures; unquantified downside
- **What they want**: Confidence that management has the situation under control

### Technical Mode (Leadership Team, Engineering, Product)

- **Lead with**: Problem statement + proposed solution
- **Quantify**: Where relevant; can also use qualitative rationale
- **Risk framing**: Trade-offs — acknowledge complexity
- **Language**: Can use domain terminology; explain if cross-functional
- **Structure**: Context → Problem → Options considered → Recommended path → Dependencies
- **What they fear**: Being asked to do something without the "why"
- **What they want**: Enough context to execute without further questions

### Operational Mode (All-staff, Suppliers)

- **Lead with**: "What this means for you" — the personal impact
- **Quantify**: Specific dates, dollar amounts where relevant; avoid percentages without context
- **Risk framing**: Acknowledge concerns directly; do not dismiss
- **Language**: Plain English, no jargon, contractions acceptable
- **Structure**: Impact on reader → Why this is happening → What changes → What stays the same → What to do next
- **What they fear**: Uncertainty about their own situation
- **What they want**: Clarity and reassurance (or honest acknowledgement of difficulty)

### Customer Mode

- **Lead with**: Benefit or reassurance
- **Quantify**: Only if it helps them (e.g. "your price increases by $5/month")
- **Risk framing**: Acknowledge inconvenience; do not over-promise
- **Language**: Warm, plain, customer-facing (no internal language)
- **Structure**: What this means for you (good news first) → What's changing → When → What you need to do → How to get help
- **What they fear**: Price increases, service degradation, being abandoned
- **What they want**: To feel valued and informed, not surprised

### Regulatory Mode (ASIC, ATO, Fair Work Commission, etc.)

- **Lead with**: Regulatory context and obligation being addressed
- **Quantify**: Precise amounts, dates, reference numbers
- **Risk framing**: Precise — cite the relevant section of the relevant act
- **Language**: Formal, passive voice acceptable, no contractions
- **Structure**: Regulatory basis → Facts → Action taken / proposed → Timeline → Contact for queries
- **What they fear**: Non-compliance, ambiguity, missing disclosure
- **What they want**: Explicit compliance, documented evidence, clear accountability

## Tone Matrix

| Tone | Use for | Characteristics | Avoid |
|------|---------|-----------------|-------|
| **Formal** | Board, Investors, Regulators | Full sentences, no contractions, titles, precise | Casual language, emoji, first-person plural overuse |
| **Direct** | Leadership team | Short paras, active voice, bullets, contractions OK | Excessive caveats, padding, passive voice |
| **Warm** | All-staff, Customers | Empathetic opener, acknowledges impact, "we" language | Clinical distance, dismissing concerns, corporate speak |
| **Regulatory** | ASIC, ATO, FWC | Citations, precise obligations, zero ambiguity | Vague commitments, informal phrasing |

## Confidentiality Labels

| Label | Meaning | When to use |
|-------|---------|------------|
| **Public** | May be shared externally without restriction | Customer comms, press releases |
| **Internal** | For employees only | All-staff updates, internal policy changes |
| **Confidential** | For named recipients only | Board packs, investor updates, legal matters |
| **Board Confidential** | Board and executive team only | M&A, litigation, strategic pivots |

## Brief Length and Format Guide

| Audience | Target word count | Format preference |
|----------|------------------|------------------|
| Board | 400–600 words | Table + bullets; no long prose paragraphs |
| Investors | 500–800 words | Narrative + data table; formal headers |
| Leadership team | 500–900 words | Mix of prose and bullets; can include appendix reference |
| All-staff | 400–700 words | Short paragraphs, FAQ at end if complex topic |
| Customers | 300–500 words | Short paragraphs only; single CTA at end |
| Suppliers | 400–600 words | Formal structure; include contractual reference if relevant |

## Common Brief Topics and Key Facts Required

| Topic | Mandatory facts |
|-------|----------------|
| Price increase | New price, effective date, % change, rationale, grandfathering policy |
| Financial results | Period, revenue, EBITDA or net profit, vs prior period, vs budget |
| Product change | Feature affected, effective date, customer impact, alternatives |
| Restructure | Roles affected, timeline, support offered, next communication point |
| Acquisition / investment | Parties, deal terms (if disclosable), strategic rationale, integration timeline |
| Regulatory compliance | Regulation cited, action taken, effective date, ongoing obligations |
| Strategy change | Old direction, new direction, why, what changes operationally |
