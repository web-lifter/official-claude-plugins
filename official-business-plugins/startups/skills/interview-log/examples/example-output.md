# Interview 003 — P3, Head of Legal, ~120-staff edtech (Brisbane)

- Date: 2026-04-30
- Duration: 58 minutes
- Recording: yes (audio + Otter transcript; original kept off-repo)
- Interviewee: P3 (pseudonym), Head of Legal, ~120-staff edtech, Brisbane
- Channel: Zoom; introduced by Priya Natarajan (ACC Australia chapter contact)

## Raw notes

Priya: *(intro, recording permission, 30-min target — actual ran 58 min as P3 was happy to keep going)*

P3: "Last MSA I did? Tuesday. A US-based content vendor wanted us to redistribute their courseware in our K–12 packages. Forty-three pages. Landed in my inbox at 3:40pm with a 'we'd love to sign Thursday'. I worked through it from about 4 until 9-something with a break for dinner."

Priya: "What did you have open while you were working through it?"

P3: "Word, with the document. Excel — I have this colour-coded spreadsheet of standard redlines we've accepted before, by clause type. ChatGPT in a private browser window but I won't paste contract content into it, I just use it for general 'what does this phrasing usually mean'. Slack open because the partnerships lead kept pinging. And my own personal notebook with a running list of 'buyer shall…' clauses."

Priya: "Walk me through the actual review."

P3: "I start with indemnity, liability cap, governing law, termination. Those are my critical four. Then I read forward. The thing that kills me — every time — is the auto-renewal language hidden in section 14 or wherever. I have this paranoia about auto-renewals because two years ago at my old company I missed one and we got auto-billed about AU$78,000 for the next year. I had to write that note to the CFO. So now I'm extra paranoid and it adds time."

Priya: "Have you tried any AI tools for this?"

P3: "I had a Spellbook trial. It's good. But it's trained on US contracts and the corpus shows — it doesn't flag PPSA stuff, it doesn't pick up modern slavery reporting language, and the indemnity recommendations are American-shaped. I gave it three weeks and dropped it. ChatGPT — same problem, plus I'm not pasting client contract text into a public model, that's a non-starter."

Priya: "If I waved a wand and a tool could give you a redline plus a one-page negotiation brief in 20 minutes for a 40-page MSA, what would you do with the time?"

P3: "Honestly? I'd go home. Or I'd do the obligation tracking properly. That spreadsheet I keep — it should not be a spreadsheet. After signature I genuinely have no system, and finance discovers things three months later that I should have flagged."

Priya: "What would have to be true for you to trust the redline?"

P3: "I'd need to see it on a contract I know cold. Run it on the last six MSAs I did and let me compare. If it catches what I caught and flags things I didn't, I'd trust it within a week. If it misses something — even one thing — I'm spending the whole 20 minutes re-reading anyway, so the value's gone."

Priya: "What would a tool like this cost in your world?"

P3: "Spellbook was around — I'd have to check — I think around AU$220 USD a month? It's not cheap. But if we're talking three hours back per week, the maths is easy at $300, $400 a seat. The blocker isn't price, it's *me* signing off. Procurement holds the budget — Megan, our Head of Procurement. She and I would both need to be happy."

Priya: "Why haven't you solved this yourself already?"

P3: "Because every option I've tried is built for American lawyers. And because I'm one person, the head-down work doesn't leave time to evaluate tools properly. I tried Spellbook on stolen evenings. That's not how you evaluate software."

Priya: "Who else should I talk to?"

P3: "Megan — I'll intro you. And there's a guy at one of the Sydney health-techs Priya you'd know — actually if you don't, I'll DM you. And the ACC Australia mid-market chat has about twenty people in our band — happy to post in there if you want, with your permission."

Priya: "Can we follow up in a few weeks once I've sketched something?"

P3: "Yes — show me anything visual and I'll give you a frank read. Don't make me read a deck."

## Findings

### Day-in-the-life

- A 43-page MSA can land at 3:40pm and require Thursday sign-off — observed in P3's most recent Tuesday review.
- Stack used during review: Word + Excel (personal redline-precedent sheet) + ChatGPT (carefully, no contract content) + Slack + paper notebook for obligations.
- "Critical four" reading order: indemnity, liability cap, governing law, termination.
- Personal pain history shapes review behaviour: previous AU$78k auto-renewal incident adds time to every review.

### Problem deep-dive

- Spellbook trialled for 3 weeks; dropped because US-corpus misses PPSA, modern slavery reporting, AU-shaped indemnity.
- ChatGPT used for generic clause-interpretation help only; never for pasted contract content (confidentiality non-starter).
- Post-signature obligation tracking is a personal Excel spreadsheet — explicit "should not be a spreadsheet" admission.

### Solution probe

- "I'd go home" / "I'd do obligation tracking properly" — both gains land hard.
- Trust threshold: run the tool on the last 6 MSAs reviewed; must match P3's catches and add new ones; *zero misses* tolerated.
- Pricing inside frame at AU$300–AU$400/seat/month. Procurement (Megan) is the budget-holder; co-sign with legal.

### Close

- Referrals: introduction to Megan (procurement Head) + a Sydney health-tech GC + permission to post in ACC Australia mid-market chat.
- Follow-up: yes, on visual prototype, "don't make me read a deck."
