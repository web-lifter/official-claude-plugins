# references — swot-build

The SWOT framework — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats — and especially the **internal vs external distinction** is credited to:

- **Humphrey, Albert S.** SOFT analysis (later renamed SWOT). Stanford Research Institute, 1960s. — Strengths and Weaknesses are properties **internal** to the organisation; Opportunities and Threats are forces **external** to it.

The distinction matters because it determines the response:

- An **internal Strength** (e.g. "deep AU/NZ legal-corpus training data") is something the venture controls — it can be invested in or doubled down on.
- An **external Opportunity** (e.g. "Modern Slavery Act 2018 reporting obligations driving demand for contract-clause review") is something the venture can ride but cannot create.

Confusing the two (a common failure mode — "great brand" listed as an Opportunity) leads to ambiguous strategy because the response confuses *act on yourself* with *position against the market*.

The "What it means for us" insight section in each SWOT closes the loop back to the venture's own hypothesis register — every quadrant should yield at least one candidate hypothesis or strategic action.
