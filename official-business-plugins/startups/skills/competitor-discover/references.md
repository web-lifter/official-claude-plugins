# references — competitor-discover

The five-source taxonomy of competitor discovery — direct, indirect, substitute, current workaround, do-nothing — is consistent with the discovery practice in:

- **Blank, Steve & Dorf, Bob.** *The Startup Owner's Manual.* K&S Ranch, 2012. — Phase 1 (customer discovery) treats competitors as part of the market hypothesis.
- **Maurya, Ash.** *Running Lean* (3rd ed.). O'Reilly, 2022. — the lean canvas's "Existing Alternatives" cell is the direct + workaround + do-nothing categories combined.
- **Christensen, Clayton M.; Hall, Taddy; Dillon, Karen; Duncan, David S.** *Competing Against Luck.* Harper Business, 2016. — the JTBD lens reframes substitutes (a spreadsheet substitutes for a CRM if the underlying job is "track who I last talked to").

The "do-nothing" category is the most under-counted competitor in early-stage discovery. It captures the *status quo* — what the segment is paying (in time, money, or risk) to leave the problem unsolved. For B2B early-stage ventures, do-nothing usually wins more deals than the named direct competitor.

The skill drives candidate discovery through five complementary research surfaces:

1. Trade publications and industry analyst lists
2. Ad-network and SEO surfacing (paid keywords for the same job-to-be-done)
3. Conference exhibitor and speaker lists
4. Peer recommendations from interview subjects ("who else have you looked at?")
5. Job-to-be-done search queries (search by job, not by product category)
