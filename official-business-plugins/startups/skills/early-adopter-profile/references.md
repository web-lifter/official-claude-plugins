# early-adopter-profile — references

The five-criteria earlyvangelist definition this skill enforces comes from Steve Blank's customer-development model. See [`startups/SOURCES.md`](../../../SOURCES.md) for full citations.

## The five criteria

- **Blank, Steve & Dorf, Bob.** *The Startup Owner's Manual.* K&S Ranch, 2012.
  - Source of the conjunctive five-criteria test: an earlyvangelist (1) has the problem, (2) knows they have it, (3) is actively looking for a solution, (4) has cobbled together a workaround, and (5) has budget. Blank's claim is that prospects scoring 5/5 are the *most reliable test population* a startup can find — they will buy on incomplete evidence and evangelise on completion.
- **Blank, Steve.** *The Four Steps to the Epiphany.* K&S Ranch, 2005.
  - Earlier framing of the same lens. A customer who scores 4/5 is still valuable but is *not* an earlyvangelist; treating them as one biases the discovery sample.

## Why all five — not most five

The criteria are conjunctive because each one removes a different distortion in the validation sample. A prospect who has the problem but does not know they have it will need education before they can give useful feedback. A prospect who knows the problem but has not cobbled a workaround is not motivated enough to switch. A prospect with no budget cannot signal willingness to pay. Treating any of these as optional inflates the apparent strength of the validation signal.

## Three per primary segment — the bar for 🟢

`customer-discovery-status` Q3 requires ≥ 3 named earlyvangelists with 5/5 criteria and ≥ 2× engagement. Fewer than three is yellow; the venture cannot move to MVP-planning work on a sample of one or two. This is consistent with Blank's broader insistence on a sample large enough to support a *decision*, not just a story.
